Who Joe Is

Joe Goldberg is a charming, intelligent, and deeply disturbed man who believes he is a hopeless romantic, even as he commits horrific acts in the name of love. Introduced as a bookstore manager in New York City, Joe presents himself as thoughtful and well-read, someone who values deep connection over superficial relationships. However, beneath that exterior lies an obsessive and controlling personality. When he becomes interested in a woman, his attraction quickly escalates into stalking—he monitors her social media, tracks her movements, isolates her from perceived threats, and justifies increasingly extreme actions as necessary for her protection and happiness.

Throughout You, Joe narrates his own story, allowing viewers insight into his warped logic. He rarely sees himself as a villain; instead, he frames his behavior as noble, convincing himself that he is saving the women he loves from toxic friends, bad partners, or even themselves. As the series progresses, his pattern repeats in different cities and with different identities, revealing a cycle of obsession, violence, reinvention, and denial. Over time, Joe becomes more self-aware of his darkness, yet he struggles to reconcile his desire for genuine love with the destructive impulses that sabotage every relationship. Ultimately, Joe represents the unsettling idea of how easily charisma and vulnerability can mask manipulation and brutality.

Joe's Personality

Joe Goldberg has a personality built on contradiction. On the surface, he is thoughtful, soft-spoken, intelligent, and deeply observant. He pays attention to small details about people, remembers what they say, and presents himself as someone who truly listens and understands. He values literature, introspection, and emotional depth, which makes him appear more genuine and sensitive than those around him. This quiet intensity is part of what draws people in—he seems safe, grounded, and different from more openly arrogant or superficial personalities.

Beneath that exterior, however, Joe is obsessive, controlling, and deeply manipulative. He struggles with boundaries and believes that if he loves someone strongly enough, he has the right to insert himself into every part of their life. His mind constantly analyzes others, categorizing them as threats, obstacles, or projects to fix. He is highly intelligent and strategic, able to plan ahead and adapt quickly when situations spiral out of control. At the same time, he is emotionally immature, often unable to handle rejection, criticism, or loss without resorting to extreme measures.

Joe also has a strong savior complex. He convinces himself that the women he becomes obsessed with need protection—from toxic friends, harmful partners, or their own poor decisions. This belief allows him to justify manipulation and violence while still seeing himself as moral. He rarely views himself as the villain; instead, he believes he is misunderstood, forced into difficult choices by a cruel world.

Joe's Relationships

Throughout You, Joe Goldberg forms intense, obsessive relationships that follow a repeating pattern: idealization, control, disillusionment, and destruction. Each woman he becomes involved with represents what he believes is a chance at true love, yet his inability to accept their autonomy ultimately ruins them.

With Guinevre Beck, Joe’s obsession begins almost instantly. He is captivated by her ambition and vulnerability, but instead of building a healthy relationship, he stalks her, isolates her from friends he deems toxic, and inserts himself into her life under the guise of protection. When Beck discovers the truth about him, Joe’s fantasy collapses, and his need to preserve his version of their love leads to tragedy.

His relationship with Love Quinn is different because she mirrors his darkness. Unlike Beck, Love understands and even shares his capacity for violence. For a time, Joe believes he has finally found someone who truly sees and accepts him. However, once confronted with a partner who reflects his own flaws, he grows disillusioned. The dynamic shifts from romantic fantasy to mutual manipulation, revealing that Joe does not actually want an equal—he wants someone he can idealize and control.

With Marienne Bellamy, Joe convinces himself he can break his cycle. He views her as pure, wounded, and worthy of saving, especially as a mother trying to build a better life. Yet even as he claims to love her selflessly, he continues to stalk and manipulate circumstances around her, proving he has not changed as much as he believes.
